Musee de l'Orangerie

The Musee de l'Orangerie is an Impressionist and Post-Impressionist painting art galley. It's located in the west corner of the Tuileries Garden, next to the Place de la Concorde. It is recognized for the permanent display of Claude Monet's eight large "Water Lilies" murals.

The Water Lilies murals were amazing. I actually did not do any research on the Musee de l'Orangerie, so I was surprised to see its collection in the lower level. There were many amazing collections, but I guess I was too focused on looking at the paintings that I didn't get many photos!

My Favorites

Of course. Water Lilies comes in first for my favorites at the Musee de l'Orangerie, but there were still other good pieces!

#1: Claude Monet, Water Lilies, 1920-1926

Surprisingly, this was my favorite. I definitely think seeing the paintings up close in real life changed my perception of them.

#2: Claude Monet, Water Lilies, 1920-1926

Similar to the first painting, seeing this painting up close made me admire it more. The painting appears to be somewhat of a smooth surface, but it's not like a licked surface type of smooth. When you look at it closer, you can see the handling of the paint. What I liked the most when looking at it was the puddle anf reflectioon between the stones- it's really amazing when you look at it in person.

#3: Pablo Picasso, Seated Harlequin with Red Background, 1905

I like basically any painting with a licked surface. This one's very interesting. She's laying on a wave in a relaxed posed, yet I can't imagine a way to comfortably lay in such a postion as she does.

#4: Pablo Picasso, Nature morte sur un piano, 1911

This was one of the paintings I remembered from the Art History class I took my very first semester. I'm not sure why I was always drawn to it. Of course, the color pallette is very pleasing, and the hazy effect of the smoke makes it feel like you're experiencing the exact moment he painted. I was very excited when I realized I was at the station when I recognized the structure.
